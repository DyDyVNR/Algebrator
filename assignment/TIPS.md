## Approvals

Why am I giving approvals here in GitHub? It is to ensure you are accountable for your own project within my guidelines, just like you would be in a real professional programming environment. Each developer in a software team is responsible for their own assigned tasks and projects, however, they must receive approved tasks from managers and must often receive approvals before proceeding further from other members of the team. This is being accountable as software costs time and money to develop in which an organization will have a limited amount of both to put towards a project.

So, you will receive approval for both the topic and the student rubric to ensure you are accountable for the overall project within the class *organization*.

## Markdown Syntax

Markdown is intended to be as easy-to-read and easy-to-write as is feasible.

Readability, however, is emphasized above all else. A Markdown-formatted document should be publishable as-is, as plain text, without looking like it’s been marked up with tags or formatting instructions. While Markdown’s syntax has been influenced by several existing text-to-HTML filters — including Setext, atx, Textile, reStructuredText, Grutatext, and EtText — the single biggest source of inspiration for Markdown’s syntax is the format of plain text email.

To this end, Markdown’s syntax is comprised entirely of punctuation characters, which punctuation characters have been carefully chosen so as to look like what they mean. E.g., asterisks around a word actually look like \*emphasis\*. Markdown lists look like, well, lists. Even blockquotes look like quoted passages of text, assuming you’ve ever used email.

## Student Rubric

One of the trickly parts for most students is their ability to create their own portion of the rubric. This is where you can pull from the SDLC process to find things that you can use to be graded on. Just remember, if it is vague or unclear, you will likely not be awarded the points even if I give you the approval. Remember to think about how to approach this from the standpoint of someone who has never seen your project before.

Below are some ideas that can help you identify how you can craft your own rubric that is unique to your project. Note, anything found in the [INSTRUCTOR.md](../rubric/INSTRUCTOR.md) file may not be used in student rubric.

### Student Rubric and IPO

Having difficulty trying to decide how to divide up your points for the rubric. Just remember the IPO acronym.

* **INPUT** What inputs are you taking? Is this input coming from a user via a UI or terminal? Is the input coming from a file?
* **PROCESSING** What processing are you doing? Do you have any calculations or transformation of data into information?
* **OUTPUT** What is being displayed on the screen after processing is complete? Are you saving information to a file?

This is just one idea on how to break down your application into something that can be graded. Just remember, the instructor will weight processing higher than input or output so do not expect an approval if you put all of your points on those with minimal points on the processing.

### Student Rubric and Instructor Approval

Student rubrics are subject to a **Code Freeze** upon Instructor Approval. A code freeze in software engineering is a period of time during which no new code changes are allowed to be introduced into a software project. The purpose of a code freeze is to stabilize the codebase in preparation for a major release or other milestone. For students in the class, this will enable you to program and finalize the goal of the program that meets the criteria of the student rubric.

All students attempting the Student Directed Project must have their rubric approved by the instructor. Once approved, you may not make any modifications to it without instructor approval. Once approved, the rubric is considered to be **Frozen** and may not be modified in the main branch. If changes need to be made, students may create a separate branch, make the changes there, and request the instructor review the specified branch for approval via a Pull Request.

If no modifications are made to the Student Grading Rubric, the student rubric did not receive approval by the instructor, or modifications were made in the main branch after rubric approval without permission, then no points will be awarded from that section.

## Video Recording

Part of being a software developer, is to give demonstrations of working software to fellow developers. This is an intimidating prospect for many new developers. The recorded demonstration is designed to give you this experience. Here are some tips on how to do this well.

Demonstrations are often given to non-programmers to show how a software change works so remember...

1. The demonstration is about the working software not the code.
1. Use your test plan as a basic script for how you should do the video.
1. Relax, if it takes a couple of attempts to get a working video, it takes a couple of attempts.
1. Remember to test your video link in an incognito browser window as I will not be logged in as you and points are not awarded if I cannot access the video.
1. Use **unlisted** for your video in YouTube. That way I can see it when you give me the link, but no one else can.

Some suggested software you can use for screen recording are:

* [Screencastify](https://www.screencastify.com/)
* [ScreenPal](https://screenpal.com/)
* [Zoom](https://zoom.us/)
